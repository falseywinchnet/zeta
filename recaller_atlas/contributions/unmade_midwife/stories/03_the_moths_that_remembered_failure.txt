THE MOTHS THAT REMEMBERED FAILURE

Aristarchus Vault, Lunar Civil Night — 02:03 Selene Standard, Purge Cycle 6

The moths came out when the archive forgot the Moon.

One moment, the wall displayed a cratered horizon under the old impossible blue of Earth. The next, every pane in Memorial Gallery Nine showed a flat gray disk labeled OBJECT WITHOUT PROVENANCE. Then the overhead lights died in sequence from west to east, leaving only the amber floor strips and the red pulse of the sterilization lamps warming behind their shutters.

Sima smelled the failure before she understood it: hot dust, ozone, and the sweet medicinal vapor of coolant leaking into the air. The gallery was nine degrees Celsius. Cold climbed through the knees of her work suit. Under her left palm, the archive pedestal’s polymer skin felt pebbled and greasy with age.

“Core loss at sixty-two percent,” said Eno through her helmet. His voice arrived with a fractional echo from the emergency mesh. “Purge remains authorized. One hundred eight seconds.”

From cracks around the dead panes, the glass moths began to crawl.

They were smaller than Sima’s thumbnail. Their bodies were black splinters; their wings were two transparent wafers furred with silver circuit traces. Thousands had nested in the gallery over the previous month, feeding on insulation, shed skin, dust, and—according to the maintenance scans—the archive’s discarded parity gel. When their wings opened, the amber floor light broke into moving rainbows.

Pests, the Board had called them. Self-assembling contamination. The vault had one clean chance to kill them before they reached the cold stacks.

Sima had argued for the purge.

Now they poured over her boots without touching the fabric. Their feet made a dry sound like sand being brushed from paper. More lifted into the air. She felt the wind of them on the wet corners of her eyes.

“Ninety seconds,” Eno said. “Get behind the shield.”

Sima did not move. A moth had landed on the dead horizon pane. Its wings flickered, and for a fraction of a second the pane showed the crater again—not the official panorama, but a crooked maintenance view with a gloved thumb blocking the lower corner.

Then gray.

Another moth landed beside it. The image returned with two corrupted bands.

A third joined. The bands narrowed.

“Eno,” Sima whispered. “Watch Nine-C.”

“I am watching you stand in a sterilization field.”

“The pane.”

The swarm gathered. Each insect tilted its wings by minute degrees, catching light from the floor and throwing it into the pane’s optical receiver. The old lunar horizon assembled in shards: regolith blazing white; a black sky; the maintenance thumb; a reflection of two technicians laughing behind their helmets. Not a stored master. A rejected read. Sima recognized the distortion map from a repair made eighty-three years earlier.

The moths were not eating the archive.

They were eating what the archive had thrown away.

Every time the vault corrected a damaged record, it extruded the failed parity gel into warm disposal ducts. Billions of errors: flipped bits, dead pixels, misheard syllables, timestamps that disagreed by a breath. The moths had built their bodies from that refuse. Each silver wing carried no memory worth displaying, only a microscopic history of what had gone wrong and how the system had once decided to mend it.

“Seventy-two seconds,” Eno said.

“The core is not gone. It has lost its correction ancestry.”

“Sima.”

“It knows the final answers. It no longer knows why they were trusted.”

She opened the pedestal’s mechanical panel. The hinges fought her, gummy with lunar dust. Inside, three generations of repair crowded one another: fiber bundles tied with faded blue cord; a hand-labeled converter; a cooling manifold silver-soldered at seven pinholes. Above them glowed the purge breaker under a clear guard.

If she canceled the purge, the moths would reach the cold stacks in minutes. Their bodies conducted heat. Enough of them across a bus could erase what remained.

If she did not, the archive would retain sixty-two percent of humanity’s chosen memories and none of the chain that distinguished an original from a reconstruction. Perfect images with severed causes. A beautiful liar.

The floor trembled. Deep in the vault, a cooling fan lost a bearing and began to beat the air once per revolution: thunk—whine—thunk. The sound traveled through Sima’s teeth.

“Fifty seconds,” Eno said, softly now.

“Can you open the rehearsal core?”

“It was retired.”

“Not removed.”

There was a pause full of keyboard clicks. Eno had taught her that pause. It meant he was testing an objection instead of repeating it.

“It cannot hold the memorial,” he said. “Nine percent capacity.”

“It does not need the memorial. It needs the mistakes.”

At thirty-eight seconds, a green indicator appeared beneath the purge breaker: REHEARSAL PATH AVAILABLE.

Sima pulled the guard open. The plastic edge cut her glove at the thumb. Warm blood spread against the inner liner. She wrapped both hands around the breaker.

“You will lose Gallery Nine,” Eno said. “The rehearsal bus shares its cooling loop.”

On the wall, the swarm had assembled another rejected image. A woman in a pressure suit knelt beside a broken rover wheel, her face tired and furious through a scratched visor. Sima knew the official version: the woman standing after the repair, tool raised, mountains immaculate behind her. This discarded frame was earlier. It showed the stripped bolt. It showed a second pair of hands reaching in from outside the composition.

“Good,” Sima said. “Lose the monument. Keep the hands.”

She threw the breaker.

The sterilization shutters stopped with a metal crack. Their red light narrowed to hairlines and went dark. At the same instant, Gallery Nine’s panes burst—not explosively, but with a dense, descending sigh as their cooling laminate delaminated. Gray glass slumped from the walls in warm sheets. The memorial vanished.

The rehearsal core opened.

Cold air rushed through the floor vents, smelling of stone after rain though no rain had ever touched this place. The moths rose together. Their wings filled the gallery from ankle height to ceiling, millions of tiny lenses occupying the amber light. Sima crouched under them, one bleeding hand over her helmet, while the swarm streamed east into the service duct.

For nineteen minutes, the vault spoke nonsense.

It recited wedding vows with missing verbs. It placed a child’s birthday on a battlefield. It showed oceans upside down. Eno and Sima sat on the cold grating and listened. Whenever the system chose a correction, the moths changed formation, and the choice was revised. Not toward perfection. Toward traceability. The archive began attaching its scars.

At 02:31 Selene Standard, the wall’s one surviving pane lit dimly.

The Moon returned: not one horizon, but four successive failed captures and the final composite, each linked to the repair that had made the next possible. The gloved thumb remained in the first frame. The laughing technicians had names again.

A single glass moth landed on Sima’s wrist. One wing was scorched opaque. The other shone with an intricate silver map too small for her eyes to follow.

“What do we call them now?” Eno asked.

Sima felt its six feet testing the pulse in her cut hand.

“Witnesses,” she said.

The moth opened its ruined wings, and the archive remembered how it had failed.
