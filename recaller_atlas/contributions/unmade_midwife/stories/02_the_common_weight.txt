THE COMMON WEIGHT

Vigil of Kinship, Turn 880,104 — 220:17 Rim Time, Asterion Relay

Eleven minutes before the red sun entered the eastern window, the Children of Salt met the Children of Vacuum under one gravity.

Tesh saw them waiting at the far end of the spindle bridge: twelve narrow figures wrapped in dull silver membranes, each twice her height, their limbs folded close against the unfamiliar drag of the rotating station. The pressure veil made them waver. Behind them lay hard vacuum, black enough to reveal the old relay’s scratched window as a scratched window—mica layers, resin repairs, thumb-wide clamps—rather than an opening cut cleanly into the stars.

On Tesh’s side, the air was cool and wet. Fourteen degrees by the bridge thermometer. It smelled of iodine, machine oil, and the mineral tang of the brine pools where her people had slept during the forty-day ascent. Condensation pearled on her blue-gray skin and gathered in the soft fans along her ribs. Each breath clicked in the back of her throat. Her ceremonial coat, woven from salt-cured kelp fiber, rasped heavily at her shoulders.

“Do they look like us?” whispered Yara, her small clutch-sister.

“No,” Tesh said.

“Do we look like them?”

Across the veil, one of the silver figures tilted its long head. A movement like listening.

“No,” Tesh said, and Yara’s gill fans opened with relief.

For eighteen thousand years the two descents had answered the relay’s beacon. Salt had changed itself for the ocean under Pelagos: dense bones, pressure-folding joints, eyes shuttered against blue darkness. Vacuum had changed itself for the comet caravans: blood held in many small chambers, black photosynthetic veils, slow muscles efficient in almost no gravity. Their messages crossed the dark in compressed bursts. Their bodies had not shared a room since both were still close enough to old humanity to be lonely for it.

Between them, at the center bearing, stood the Common Weight.

It was only a stone: a fist-sized piece of anthracite carried from Earth when Earth was still a destination. Its black surface was worn satin-smooth by hands that no longer existed in any recognizable shape. For the ritual, one delegate from each descent would lift it together and hold it for sixty-three shared heartbeats. No archive would be exchanged until weight had been felt in common.

Tesh approached the veil. The station’s artificial gravity pulled at every sac in her body. Her knees burned. From the other side came Oru-of-the-Long-Apsis, his silver membrane fretted with hundreds of neat repair stitches. He moved on four jointed walking staffs. Under the veil, his face was smaller than Tesh expected, dark and delicate, his eyes sheltered behind wet crystal cups.

The pressure field counted down without words: three violet lights, two, one.

The veil vanished.

Air rushed toward Vacuum’s side. Pumps thundered beneath the grating. Oru’s membrane swelled as it stiffened against the atmosphere. Tesh smelled hot resin. They both reached for the stone.

The spindle lurched.

Gravity swung forty degrees sideways. Tesh struck the rail with her hip. Oru’s front staff snapped at an old mended socket. The Common Weight jumped from its cradle and rolled toward the open maintenance race around the bearing.

Yara caught it.

She caught it with both hands and screamed. The stone weighed almost as much as she did under the surge. Her webbed feet slid across wet grating. Beyond her heels, the race was a moving dark slot full of grease and toothed metal.

Tesh seized Yara’s coat. Oru folded around both of them, his membrane arms surprisingly strong. The three stopped with the stone half over the gap.

Then the spindle lurched again.

An alarm began as a low brass pulse felt more in bone than ear. On the center bearing, three carbon shoes had cracked. The relay’s rotation was slowing asymmetrically. If the bridge lost spin before the pressure sections sealed, Salt’s delegates would drown in their own unsupported organs and Vacuum’s would rupture in the full atmosphere.

The Keeper arrived, dragging the bearing kit. She opened its battered case. Empty sockets stared upward. “No graphite shoes.”

“Make them,” Tesh said.

“From what?”

Every eye turned to the Common Weight.

The Keeper recoiled. “That object crossed Sol.”

The brass pulse quickened. Fine frost formed at the far edge of the pressure veil. Oru’s membrane had split at one elbow; clear sealant oozed from a self-repair blister. Yara still held the stone. In its polished black surface, the first red light from the eastern window appeared: a line thin as a fresh cut.

The ritual law was older than either body in the room. The Common Weight must not be divided. It was the final unduplicated matter of their beginning.

Oru raised one trembling staff and touched the stone.

His translator spoke through the bridge speakers, using a voice modeled long ago on falling rain. “If beginning is undivided,” it said, “no one can use it.”

Tesh looked at Yara. The child’s arms shook. Saltwater tears were standing in the hollows beneath her shuttered eyes.

“How many shoes?” Tesh asked.

“Three.”

“Then make four.”

She took the hammer from the bearing kit.

The first blow made a small sound. That was the terrible part. Eighteen thousand years, nine extinguished habitats, two changed species, and the end of Earth’s last carried stone sounded like a cook cracking a seed.

The anthracite split along a silver seam.

The second blow broke it into workable pieces.

Everyone moved then. The Keeper ground fragments against the ribbed underside of the kit. Oru mixed the black powder with vacuum resin extruded from his own sleeve. Tesh packed the paste into the shoe molds with her thumbs. Yara pressed the fourth mold although no one had asked her. Their fluids mingled in the carbon: brine, sealant, sweat.

They installed the shoes while the bearing turned. Metal teeth passed a finger’s width from Tesh’s knuckles, trailing heat. Oru braced her against the sideways gravity. The Keeper drove each shoe home with the heel of the same hammer. The fourth went into the oldest socket, where three prior repairs had been drilled out and drilled again.

At 220:28 Rim Time, the spindle steadied.

The red sun entered from the east at a grazing angle, passed through the layered window, and filled the bridge with dim garnet light. It showed everything the white work lamps had hidden: pores in Tesh’s skin, dust in Oru’s stitches, grease under Yara’s nails, black fingerprints on every silver staff. The air pumps slowed to a deep, domestic hum.

There was no stone left to lift.

Tesh opened her hand. On her palm lay one anthracite splinter, no larger than a fish scale. Oru placed one long finger over it. Yara put her hand over his. Around them, the delegates joined in, Salt and Vacuum, until the fragment could no longer be seen.

They counted sixty-three shared heartbeats. Some were fast. Some came only once for every seven of the others. The translators stopped trying to average them.

Afterward, the archives opened.

Behind the walls, the four new shoes carried their common weight.
