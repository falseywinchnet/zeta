## 01 — The Ember Under the Bison

Use case: historical-scene
Asset type: cinematic landscape background for a fantastic deep-past story
Primary request: Photograph the instant in a late Upper Palaeolithic limestone cave when a soot-streaked young woman has just coaxed a tiny yellow flame from a shallow stone lamp and reunited with her younger brother behind a stalagmite. Both siblings are unharmed and fully clothed. The flame reveals several ochre hand stencils and one powerful charcoal bison drawing following the bulge of the cave wall. This is intimate family relief, not pageantry.
Scene/backdrop: A low, cold limestone chamber before sunrise in late winter. Wet walls, gritty sand, one constricted crawl fissure behind the young woman, droplets on calcite, an old black hearth rim, very sparse period-credible objects. A narrow fissure at far right breathes a trace of cold predawn blue light. No modern excavation equipment, ropes, torches, metal, woven textiles, fantasy architecture, or anachronistic objects.
Subject: The young woman is kneeling left of center, holding a shallow limestone fat lamp low in both hands. Her face shows exhaustion, soot, and fierce relief. She wears plausibly simple hide and sinew garments, practical and irregular, without glamorous styling. Her fully clothed pre-teen brother sits safely beneath a reindeer-hide wrap behind the central stalagmite, awake and looking at her. Keep anatomy, scale, soot, dirt, and clothing natural. No wounds and no heroic pose.
Composition/framing: Wide 16:9 environmental frame. Camera is inside the chamber at a child's seated height, three-quarter view toward the pair and painted wall. The bison occupies the upper-right stone bulge and must read as ancient charcoal pigment integrated into irregular rock, not a modern mural. Use the stalagmite to separate the siblings while the flame visually joins them. Preserve deep darkness beyond the lamp and negative space in the cave roof.
Lighting/mood: The limestone lamp is the primary practical source, warm amber and low, casting upward light across faces and grazing the wall texture. Extremely faint cold blue predawn enters from the far fissure as a secondary edge light. Deep, detailed shadows; smoke filament in still air; moisture catches only a few highlights. Terrible constriction and magnificent relief in one instant.
Color palette: Charcoal black, wet limestone gray, ochre red, bone and hide brown, one warm yellow flame, a trace of icy blue.
Constraints: Period-credible, nonviolent, and physically restrained. Exactly two people, both unharmed and fully clothed. The brother is clearly safe and sheltered. The woman holds a small stone lamp, not a modern candle or torch. The ancient art is sparse and believable. No readable writing.
Avoid: fantasy costumes, cave-person caricature, clean studio skin, oversized fire, extra people, dinosaurs, metal tools, pottery, dramatic magical glow, painted-looking concept art, archaeological museum lighting.

The camera is physically present in real picture space 0.85 meters above the cave floor, angled 8 degrees upward from six feet away. Use a plausible full-frame CMOS camera with a 28 mm prime lens at f/2.8, 1/40 second, ISO 2500, focus distance 2.1 meters with natural shallow-to-moderate depth falloff, 3600 K white balance, and slight protective underexposure for the flame. Practical light sources only: the limestone fat lamp and weak predawn fissure light, with physically credible smoke and humid-air scattering. Preserve natural stone, pigment, hide, hair, skin, sweat, and soot texture; restrained highlight rolloff, open but genuinely dark shadow detail, subtle lens vignetting and longitudinal chromatic aberration, fine high-ISO sensor noise. ## 02 — The Light Beneath the Atlantic

Use case: historical-scene
Asset type: cinematic landscape background for an overlooked nineteenth-century moment
Primary request: Photograph the instant at 2:26 a.m. in January 1867 when a soaked young telegraph clerk in a remote stone Atlantic cable station sees a faint repeat signal move the projected light spot of a Thomson-style mirror galvanometer. He has paused over a penciled message because one exact word separates death from survival. The drama is concentrated in his face, hand, and the tiny moving light—not in spectacle.
Scene/backdrop: Compact, historically plausible cable hut above the Irish Atlantic in a violent winter gale. Rough limewashed stone, rain streaming across two dark windows, rafters shuddering, damp plank floor, practical paraffin lamp, brass-and-wood telegraph apparatus, wire, paper scale, ivory key, water glass vibrating on the table, peat stove giving a trace of smoke. The mirror galvanometer must function visually as a lamp beam reflected by a tiny suspended mirror onto a scale, not as a later dial meter. No electric bulbs, plastics, modern insulated cable, screens, or twentieth-century equipment.
Subject: One clerk in a wet 1860s waistcoat and rolled shirtsleeves, narrow and tired, leaning forward with pencil suspended above a ruled but unreadable message form. His right hand rests near the sending key. Wind-chilled skin, damp hair, sweat at the collar, split knuckle, total attention. He is not posing and does not look at camera.
Composition/framing: Wide 16:9 environmental frame from behind and slightly to the clerk's left, showing his three-quarter profile, galvanometer apparatus center-right, shaded paraffin lamp left, and the pale projected spot on the scale as the brightest small detail. The black west window should imply the unseen ocean; rain direction and warped reflections should communicate the gale. Keep the scene uncluttered enough that apparatus, face, hand, and light form one readable chain.
Lighting/mood: Warm paraffin practical from camera-left, faint dull-red peat glow behind, cold desaturated storm light barely leaking through wet windows. A narrow optical beam crosses a little haze and lands on the scale. The flame is physically modest, reflections on brass restrained, no theatrical lightning. Mood: isolation, epistemic suspense, a continent's grief balanced on a crumb of light.
Color palette: Peat black, wet stone gray-green, dark walnut, aged brass, paraffin amber, paper cream, cold Atlantic blue-black.
Constraints: One person only. Historically plausible 1867 room and instrument. The projected spot and its optical path must be clear. Any marks on paper and scale are non-legible. No visible external sea inside the room.

Practical light sources only: shaded paraffin lamp, tiny peat glow, and storm-filtered window light, with physically credible lamp-haze and humid-air scattering. Preserve natural wet wool, skin, hair, aged brass, wood, paper, stone, condensation, rain, and smoke texture; restrained highlight rolloff, open but genuinely dark shadow detail, subtle barrel distortion and lens flare near the lamp, fine high-ISO sensor noise. ## 03 — The Eight-Legged Morning

Use case: photorealistic-natural
Asset type: cinematic extreme-macro landscape background for a fantastic ecological story
Primary request: Make a scientifically respectful extreme-macro photograph of a tardigrade newly rehydrated in a shallow rain film among roof moss at 6:11 a.m., with a nematode approaching through the same microscopic water pocket. The animal is not a monster: it is translucent, blunt-bodied, roughly half a millimeter long, with four pairs of short lobopod legs and fine claws, moving beneath a moss leaf. Wonder and danger arise from real scale and water physics.
Scene/backdrop: A north-facing patch of moss on weathered slate immediately after the first storm in a long drought. Moss leaves are a few millimeters tall, green and translucent at the edges, with quartz grit, pollen, bacterial haze, one tiny corrugated moth wing scale like a silver sail, and a curved water-air boundary overhead. The slate beyond is iron-gray and wet. No fantastical jungle, human-sized objects, giant droplets, or invented microorganisms.
Subject: One anatomically plausible tardigrade in side three-quarter view, partly protected under a real moss leaf, claws gripping the ribbed plant surface, mouth stylets subtly visible through the body, green food tint faintly inside. One slender nematode in the mid-background, ten times longer but mostly receding out of frame, bending like a muscular question mark. The tardigrade remains the clear story-critical focus. No face, eyes, teeth, expression, armor, or anthropomorphic emotion.
Composition/framing: Wide 16:9 microscopic environmental frame at about 5:1 reproduction. The tardigrade sits near the lower-left third and faces right toward a brighter passage between moss blades. The nematode arcs through the upper-right midground. The water film creates a luminous curved ceiling; the low eastern light rakes left-to-right through cells and grit. Keep scale coherent and depth falloff optical, not painted blur.
Lighting/mood: Natural blue-white post-storm sky fill with the first low sun entering from the east, warm at a grazing six-degree angle. Tiny highlights rim the eastern sides of droplets while west sides fall soft gray. Moss chloroplasts glow by transmission; quartz produces only restrained spectral points. No bioluminescence, fantasy aura, or studio ring-light reflection.
Color palette: Living moss green, slate iron-gray, water clear-blue, translucent pearl body, silver moth scale, a narrow warm-gold edge.
Constraints: The tardigrade must be visibly microscopic and anatomically grounded. One tardigrade and one nematode only as prominent animals. Real capillary-water behavior, real wet plant and mineral texture, no text.
Avoid: giant kaiju tardigrade, cartoon eyes, claws like talons, caterpillar anatomy, excessive segmentation, alien monster styling, neon microscopy colors, scanning-electron monochrome, infographic labels, impossible underwater bubbles, CGI gloss.

The camera is physically present in real picture space 7 centimeters above the slate and angled 12 degrees above the water-film plane, mounted securely to a macro rail. Use a plausible full-frame CMOS camera with a 25 mm 2.5–5x ultra-macro lens at 5:1, effective f/8, 1/160 second, ISO 1000, focus distance at the lens's working distance with a single physically coherent razor-thin focal plane across the tardigrade's front claws and trunk and natural foreground/background falloff, 4850 K white balance, and conservative exposure that retains water highlights. Practical light sources only: low eastern sun and diffuse post-storm sky, with physically credible micro-scale refraction, internal scattering through moss cells, and atmospheric softness only in the distant slate. Preserve natural cuticle translucency, moss cell walls, grit, pollen, lichen, slate, and water-surface texture; restrained highlight rolloff, credible shadow detail, subtle diffraction and edge chromatic aberration from high-magnification optics, fine sensor noise. Render a genuinely photographed instant, never concept art, illustration, stage set, videogame frame, scientific diagram, or CGI. Simulate minute spatially varying CMOS photosite phase and micro-time offsets behind the lens only as an extremely subtle vibrance cue, never visible glitching, chromatic tearing, double edges, halos, or invented objects.
