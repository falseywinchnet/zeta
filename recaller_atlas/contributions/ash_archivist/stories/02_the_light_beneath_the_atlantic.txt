FANTASTIC FICTION. The station, instrument, and period are historically plausible; the people, message, and events are invented.

THE LIGHT BENEATH THE ATLANTIC

At 2:13 in the morning on 18 January 1867, the Atlantic cable began to say that a drowned man was alive.

The message arrived in a stone hut above Foilhommerum Bay while a southwest gale leaned against Ireland. Rain came almost horizontal from the black sea, rattling the east window like thrown gravel. The lamp chimney trembled. Each gust pressed peat smoke backward down the flue and laid its oily bitterness over the smells of hot brass, damp wool, paraffin, and the salt drying white on Owen Vale's boots.

He had been alone for five hours.

On the table, the mirror galvanometer watched him with its single moving star. A shaded lamp cast a narrow beam onto a mirror smaller than a shirt button, suspended within coils of silk-covered wire. The mirror threw the beam onto a paper scale three feet away. When current came through the copper heart of the cable, the spot leaned left or right. Under two thousand miles of winter ocean, a hand in Newfoundland was moving this crumb of light.

Owen bent close enough that the scale warmed his face. Behind him, the western window was pure darkness. He hunched in a wet waistcoat while the light spot traveled like a captive moon.

The clerk at Heart's Content had sent weather, two commodity quotations, and a shipping notice. Then came an address for Knightstown.

MRS ELLEN KEEGAN STOP LOWER CHAPEL LANE STOP

Owen set his pencil to the ruled form. The cable's signals were tired tonight. The spot did not jump; it sighed. Rain struck the roof so hard that the rafters ticked.

BRIG MARIAN FOUND DISMASTED FORTY FOUR NORTH STOP

He stopped breathing.

Every house near the harbor knew the Marian. She had sailed for Saint John in September and disappeared. Seven men from the island had been aboard. After twelve weeks, Father Brennan had said the names before the altar. Ellen Keegan had stood without crying while the chapel bell counted her husband's twenty-nine years.

SURVIVORS LANDED HALIFAX STOP PATRICK KEEGAN

The beam drifted left. Right. Left twice.

Owen wrote LOST.

Thunder arrived through the floor, not the sky. A wave had struck the cliffs below, and the table's water glass drew a ring on the wood. The spot steadied at zero.

He stared at the four letters.

The difference between LOST and LIVES was not one mark but a family of small deflections, each weakened by distance, leakage, salt, and the nervous hands of two exhausted men. He replayed the signal in memory. A long right. A short left. Or had the gale moved the lamp? The paraffin flame leaned whenever the door shook; a moving source could imitate a moving mirror.

The easy thing was to finish the form. Everyone already believed Patrick dead. Ink would merely agree with the world.

Owen tore the sheet in half.

He closed the lamp's air vent until the flame shortened and stopped dancing. The room dimmed to amber edges. He wedged felt beneath the lamp base, checked the galvanometer's zero, then pressed the key.

REPEAT LAST WORD STOP SIGNAL UNCERTAIN STOP

The key's ivory knob was warm from his fingers. He waited.

Outside, at 2:21, the gale reached its height. Wind tore a slate free. It skated down the roof and shattered in the yard. Spray passed over the cliff and struck the west window, although the window stood more than a hundred feet above the bay. The hut cooled as if a door had opened into the ocean. Owen's right calf cramped from standing. Sweat collected under his collar despite the four-degree air.

Nothing moved on the scale.

He imagined the cable on the abyssal plain: copper inside gutta-percha, jute, and iron, crossing a country without dawn. Pressure and cold held it. Blind creatures moved above the buried sentence.

At 2:26 the star stirred.

REPEATING STOP PATRICK KEEGAN LIVES STOP L I V E S STOP INJURED HAND ONLY STOP

Owen wrote every deflection before allowing himself to understand it. He asked for the word once more. It returned exact.

He pulled on his greatcoat and covered the instrument. Regulations permitted the message to wait for the morning messenger. Morning was four hours away.

The rain hit him sideways when he opened the door. It was barely above freezing. Westward, the sea existed only where white water broke against unseen rock. The cable hut's lamp threw a trapezoid across the yard from behind him, so his shadow stretched northeast, long and frantic, over the shining mud.

He ran.

The road to Knightstown descended between black-mossed stone walls. Water poured along the ruts. His boots slipped on dung and shale; the message inside his coat crackled each time he caught himself. Wind drove him against a wall and split his knuckles. He kept that hand over the paper.

Above the harbor he saw only three anchor lamps jerking in the dark. Masts groaned; rigging sang. The town smelled of kelp, coal smoke, fish barrels, and rain-struck earth. Behind curtains, people slept inside the old world, where the Marian was gone.

Ellen Keegan's cottage stood at the lower end of Chapel Lane. A banked fire showed red through its grate. Owen knocked at 2:49.

Nothing.

He knocked again. A baby began crying within. Floorboards creaked. The door opened the width of Ellen's face. Her hair hung loose and silvered at the temples though she was not yet thirty. She held a poker in one hand.

“What is it?”

Owen took out the form. Rain had entered along one fold, turning the paper translucent, but the words were dry.

He raised the lantern so light fell from below across both their faces. It was a cruel angle; it hollowed her eyes and showed each drop on his lashes. He could not make the sentence gentle without making it less true.

“A cable from Halifax,” he said. “The Marian was found. Patrick lives. His hand is hurt. Only his hand.”

Ellen did not move.

The poker slipped and struck the flagstone. Its iron ring was astonishingly clear beneath the storm.

“Say the exact words.”

He read them.

She put two fingers on the raised pencil tracks as though they were a pulse, then sat abruptly on the threshold. The baby cried. A kettle murmured over the coals.

Dawn came late under the storm. At 8:07, from the cable station, Owen watched a narrow opening form in the clouds to the southeast. Pale gold light crossed the bay at a low angle and touched the wet roofs one by one. The sea remained hammered lead. In the instrument room, the tiny star continued its leftward and rightward travel, bearing insurance figures, orders, griefs, wheat, births, lies, and names.

No bell rang for the moment. No newspaper would print Owen's torn first draft. In the stove, it had already become a curl of ash.

The corrected message lay in Ellen Keegan's apron pocket while she walked through the new morning, touching it at every corner.
