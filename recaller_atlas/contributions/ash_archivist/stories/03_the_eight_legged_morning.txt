THE EIGHT-LEGGED MORNING

At 6:03 on the first cool morning after one hundred and twelve rainless days, a drop of water struck the north side of a slate roof and raised the dead.

The storm had passed before dawn. In the human streets below, gutters gargled, tires hissed on wet pavement, and a delivery truck changed gears at the hill. Up on the roof those sounds arrived as long tremors through stone. The immediate world was a forest of moss less than a thumbnail wide. Its leaves, dry and furled at midnight, now opened into green blades translucent at the edges.

The sun had not cleared the opposite chimneys. Light came from the east at a low angle, reflected blue-white from the retreating cloud bank. Every drop wore a bright rim on its eastern side and a soft gray hemisphere on its west. The slate smelled of iron and old dust. Wet lichen released a faint mushroom odor into air cooled to fourteen degrees.

Inside a pocket between two moss leaves lay something smaller than the comma in a newspaper.

For nine months it had been a tun: contracted, barrel-shaped, almost without metabolism. Its eight legs were drawn inward. Sugars glassed its tissues; water departed into the drought. Summer heated the slate above fifty degrees. Winter entered every seam. The animal neither waited nor remembered. Those are expensive activities of wet bodies.

Water found it anyway.

The first film crept around the tun. Molecules crossed the folded cuticle. Proteins loosened; membranes became borders again. The body swelled—rear, trunk, blunt head. One claw flexed. Another caught the moss leaf.

At 6:11, the animal extended all eight legs.

It moved through water only a few hundredths of a millimeter deep, a clear flood roofed by air. The sun edged over the southeastern chimney and entered sideways. Moss chloroplasts blazed. Quartz grains threw rainbows. A discarded moth scale stood in the water like a silver sail.

The animal advanced by gripping, releasing, gripping. Each foot ended in claws finer than smoke scratches. Its body was transparent enough for the dark stylets in its mouth to show. Ahead, a moss cell had split during rehydration and leaked a cloud of contents. The tardigrade pressed its mouth to the wounded wall and pierced it.

At that scale, feeding was weather. Pressure changed. Particles streamed. The clear body brightened green.

Then the moss forest bent.

A nematode ten times the tardigrade's length entered from the gutter side, a muscular question mark thrashing left and right. Bacteria clouded away. Its passage turned the moth scale.

The tardigrade released the moss cell.

No alarm sounded inside it. There was only touch, chemistry, pressure, and the old machinery of contraction. Its claws closed. Its body flattened against the underside of a leaf as the nematode passed overhead. The leaf was five cells thick. Light shone through each cell wall. On the far side, the worm's mouth worked open and shut.

A sparrow landed on the ridge tile at 6:18.

To the roof world, the impact was catastrophe without a visible cause. Slate jumped. Water leapt from hollows. The moss leaf tore at its base. The tardigrade, the leaf, the nematode, three rotifers, spores, bacteria, and the silver moth scale were swept downslope in a sheet no thicker than skin.

They traveled six inches.

For the tardigrade, the journey crossed every known boundary. The north patch vanished behind a ridge of tar. The current accelerated through a canyon between slates, cold and aerated, smelling of mineral grit. Sunlight flashed and disappeared. The animal tumbled, claws closing on water. A rotifer struck it and unfurled two ciliated crowns before the flood pulled them apart.

The film reached the copper gutter.

Here the water deepened to nearly half an inch and turned brown with leaf tannin. A drowned ant rotated below the surface. Fungal threads streamed from a twig. The nematode, cast into open water, knotted itself around a grain of sand.

The tardigrade sank.

Its claws touched nothing. The surface above reflected the brightening sky so perfectly that it looked like another blue depth. The small body rotated beneath it. Oxygen was present, but the moss food, the sheltering geometry, every usable surface had gone.

At 6:21 a human hand reached from the attic window.

The woman who lived beneath the roof had woken to the sparrow pecking at the flashing. She leaned out in a gray sweater, breath smoking faintly, and cleared a plug of leaves from the gutter. Her fingers were pink with cold. A brass ring caught the sun. She did not see the life moving in the brown water against her thumbnail.

The plug came free. Water rushed toward the downspout. The tardigrade spun past the woman's hand, over the greened copper lip, and into a vertical darkness loud with falling rain. It dropped three stories in four seconds. The pipe rang. A late church clock entered the metal as four vast blows.

The water burst into an open rain barrel in the courtyard.

Morning filled the barrel from above. The sun now stood six degrees over the roofs, sending a warm gold shaft between two buildings. It struck one half of the water while the other remained blue with shade. Steam no eye could see began leaving the sunlit boards. The air smelled of soaked cedar, stone, and the sharp green of tomato vines tied along the wall.

The tardigrade settled onto a floating island of lichen torn from the roof.

The island was not its old forest. Its chemistry differed; its leaves lay flatter; unfamiliar mites occupied the shaded interior. But there was a film. There were cells to pierce. The animal found purchase, righted itself, and crawled beneath a translucent lobe while the barrel's surface rolled above.

By 10:42 the courtyard had warmed to twenty-one degrees. A child opened the kitchen door, carrying a red cup. He dipped it into the rain barrel and splashed half the water onto the tomatoes. The lichen island rocked but remained. He looked directly at it for one second. To him it was a fleck of roof dirt.

He poured the cup at the base of the smallest plant.

Below the lichen, the tardigrade fed.

At 5:36 that evening, sunlight came from the west nearly horizontal, turning the tomato hairs to fire. The roof was already drying. On the north slate, the old moss leaves curled toward another sleep, holding emptiness where one small body had lain through heat and frost.

In the barrel courtyard, shade preserved a crescent of water beneath the lichen. The animal placed its claws along a new green cell. Around it, bacteria divided. A rotifer opened its wheels. A fungal spore split its casing. None knew they had survived a fall through a human house. None knew there had been a house.

The day enlarged around them without end.
