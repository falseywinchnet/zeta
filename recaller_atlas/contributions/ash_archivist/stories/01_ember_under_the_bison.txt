THE EMBER UNDER THE BISON

Forty-seven minutes before sunrise, on the third morning after the full moon, the eastern sky had begun to blue behind the limestone ridge. The light entered the cave mouth almost level with the frozen ground, a thin iron-colored blade that reached twelve paces into the first chamber and stopped. Beyond it, everything was black.

Sela lay on her stomach with one cheek against the stone. The rock had the cold of deep water. It numbed her jaw, then her teeth. Through the crack beneath the fallen slab she heard her brother cough once.

Not again. Once.

Behind her, wet juniper snapped in the campfire and returned to smoke. The air at the cave mouth smelled of damp fur, marrow grease, and the bitter green smoke of branches that would not burn. Inside the crack came powdered limestone and the mineral chill of a place no breath had warmed.

The slab had fallen after midnight. No one had seen it move. There had been a sound like the sky splitting, then dust, then Sela's mother screaming Tarn's name. The main passage was closed from floor to ceiling. Yet while the others levered at stones too heavy to trouble, Sela had found this seam beside the wall. When she pressed her ear to it, Tarn had answered.

Now he did not.

Sela backed away. Her elbows were flayed raw from crawling; grit shone in the blood. Old Uru caught her wrist.

“The little way narrows,” he said. Frost silvered his beard. “Your shoulders may pass. Your chest may not return.”

She pulled free.

The camp's last good coal lay under ash in a shallow piece of shoulder bone. Sela nested it in dry shelf fungus, wrapped the bundle with scraped inner bark, and breathed until one point brightened from black to red. Smoke uncoiled past her face. Its heat touched the sweat above her eyes while the rest of her shivered. She covered the coal with a perforated shell of mammoth bone and tied it beneath her chin. In one hand she took the little limestone lamp, empty except for a clot of rancid fat and a twist of moss.

At the crack she turned sideways. The dawn blade from the east lit the left side of her face and made the right side vanish. Her mother touched two fingers to Sela's lips. No blessing. No command. Just the exact pressure of skin, dry and shaking.

Sela entered.

The seam was no higher than a fox run. She moved by emptying her lungs, pushing with her toes, then stealing a breath where the roof lifted by the width of a finger. Each tap of the bone shell sent a red pulse across crystals and roots pale as grubs. Behind her, the lever stones and Uru's hoarse count diminished. Ahead, water ticked.

At the tightest place the shell caught.

Sela could neither advance nor retreat. Her left arm was pinned along her ear. Her right hand, stretched forward, found only slick stone. The coal's warmth gathered beneath her throat. Bark smoke filled the small cup before her face. Her heart struck the rock through her ribs.

She imagined Tarn, seven winters old, sitting in used-up air. Beneath the terror she found a grain of rage so clean it steadied her.

She exhaled until pain whitened her sight. With two fingers she tore the neck cord. The ember shell dropped into her palm. She pushed it ahead, scraping skin from her knuckles, then turned her head hard enough that her ear folded. The seam took her.

She slid out headfirst onto cold sand.

For several breaths she could see nothing except red branches swimming through her eyes. Then the coal showed the chamber one fragment at a time: a pale stalagmite, the black rim of an ancient hearth, ochre handprints passing along the wall. Some were broad, some missing fingers, some no bigger than Tarn's. Above them, a bison drawn in charcoal leaned into motion. Its back followed a bulge in the stone, and in the ember light it seemed to breathe.

“Sela?”

Tarn's voice came from behind the stalagmite.

She found him curled under a reindeer hide. A cut above his temple had frozen into his hair. His breath was quick and shallow, but it came. When she touched his neck, heat startled her fingers.

The fallen slab had opened another wound in the cave. Behind Tarn, a vertical fissure breathed cold air. Sela held the ember near it. The coal brightened. Somewhere beyond was morning.

She fed the coal to the moss in the lamp. For one dreadful moment the moss only smoked. Then a bead of yellow flame stood up. It threw their shadows high across the bison and put a living point in Tarn's eyes.

They followed the draft.

The fissure descended first, then turned toward the ridge. Its walls sweated. Meltwater ran into Sela's sleeves; the cold bit her wrists so sharply she gasped. Tarn crawled behind with one fist knotted in her ankle wrapping. Once the passage widened into a pit, and stones fell for three heartbeats before striking below. Sela lay across it, ribs on one ledge, toes on the other, and let Tarn crawl over her back. His knee pressed her spine. His breath smelled of blood and old berries.

The lamp shortened. Fat ran clear around the wick. From ahead came a sound too broad to be water: wind worrying the outer grass.

They reached a chimney plugged with snow. The light there was not yellow but blue, descending from directly above. Sela clawed upward. Snow packed under her nails. The crust broke without warning and poured over her face, clean and painfully bright.

At six minutes before sunrise they came out halfway up the ridge.

The valley lay violet below. To the southeast, the horizon burned beneath cloud. Smoke from the cave mouth drifted west in a gray ribbon. People spilled onto the snow when Tarn shouted. Their cries climbed the ridge, larger to Sela than thunder.

Tarn ran down. Sela remained on her knees beside the hole, the limestone lamp between her hands. Its flame looked ridiculous under the enormous brightening sky.

Then the sun's upper edge cleared the earth. Low amber light struck every ice crystal from the east and flung a million points across the valley. Mammoth tracks appeared on the distant white plain, crossing toward the river. A raven turned once above the ridge; each wingbeat flashed black, then bronze.

Sela thought of the charcoal bison moving in darkness, surrounded by hands whose owners no one could call back. She pressed her bleeding palm against the snow-crusted stone beside the opening. When she lifted it, a red print remained.

The day took the flame from the lamp by making it too small to see. It was still burning.
