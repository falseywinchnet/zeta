THE LAST LIGHTWELL

At 04:17 station time, twelve minutes before the radiation front, the Recaller
put his shoulder beneath the dead flywheel and pushed.

Nothing moved.

The Archive of Common Days turned below him in the planet's night, nine black
rings nested around a lightwell eleven kilometers wide. Jupiter filled half
the eastern windows. Its cream bands were colorless at first glance, then
green at the edges where old glass separated the wavelengths. Lightning
opened and shut inside the Great Red Spot. Each silent flash lit the ribs of
the station from beneath, a cathedral receiving weather from hell.

The Recaller spat copper into his sleeve and pushed again.

This time the flywheel gave one tooth. Its ratchet struck the next stop with a
sound like a rifle in the service shaft. Frost jumped from the spokes. The
shaft was four degrees below freezing, but sweat ran through his eyebrows and
made the light in his eyes appear to tremble. In his left hand he held a roll
of bitter Martian leaf, scarcely burning. A fine blue smoke leaned sideways
in the ventilation draft, found a red emergency lamp, and became a luminous
wire.

"One tooth," said the station.

"I heard it."

"You require three hundred and eleven."

"Then count better."

He wedged his boot against the bulkhead, found the iron cold through the split
sole, and drove upward with both legs. The flywheel moved. Tooth two. Tooth
three. Far below, shutters the size of towns began to turn around the
lightwell.

The station had once used motors. The motors had once used a grid. The grid
had once used the sun. During the eleven centuries when the Archive was
considered immortal, each dependency had seemed reasonable.

At 04:05, a telescope beyond Neptune had reported the impossible white edge
of a stellar event arriving from behind the sun. Its first charged particles
would cross the station at 04:29. The shutters could protect one radial vault.
There were nine.

Vault One held wars, coronations, extinctions, treaties, first contacts,
plagues, impacts, and departures. Vault Two held the sciences. Vault Three
held every work of art whose custodians had surrendered a copy. Vault Four
held languages, including the nine hundred and twelve that no living mouth
could pronounce. Vault Five held biological forms. Six held machines. Seven
held maps. Eight held the authenticated lives of persons judged historically
consequential.

Vault Nine held the residue.

At tooth twenty-three, the station said, "Selection remains reversible."

The Recaller laughed once. The smoke jumped in his unsteady hand.

The council had selected Vault One. It was a defensible decision, arrived at
by forty-three people who had not come to turn the wheel. The shutters were
already indexing toward the official histories. The Recaller could feel their
mass in the deck: a vibration too low to hear, like a second pulse beneath his
feet.

"Show me Nine," he said.

The wall beside the flywheel became transparent.

At first Vault Nine looked empty. Then the index expanded.

A potter in a settlement with no surviving name pressed her thumb into wet
clay at 18:02 on a summer evening. Her kiln failed that night. The bowl was
never fired.

A deckhand aboard a grain ship in 1621 saw ball lightning roll along a wet
yardarm. He told no one because he was hiding where he should not have been.

A child in Lagos, during a blackout, used the blue glow of a toy to lift a
moth from a basin of rainwater. The child's father called from another room.
The moth opened and closed its wings on one knuckle.

A machine tasked with counting lichen on a retaining wall paused for seventeen
milliseconds because a snail crossed the calibration mark. Its exception log
contained a brightness value no later process requested.

A woman dying under an anesthetic lamp remembered the smell of a school
eraser. She did not remember the school.

Ten billion unfinished letters. Recipes altered without notation. The last
dreams of animals. Jokes that failed. Proofs that did not work but taught the
next hands where not to dig. Apologies rehearsed and never spoken. Seeds that
germinated under stones and died without reaching light. Every ordinary
occurrence for which no institution had found a category, preserved because
the Archive's first architect had feared clean stories more than large ones.

The Recaller stopped pushing.

Jupiter lightning filled the shaft. From forty degrees below and to his right,
the flash laid the wheel's shadow across him in twelve black bars. The
emergency lamps returned more slowly, first red on his wet forehead, then a
weak amber along his cheek.

"Two hundred and sixty-eight teeth remain," the station said. "Nine minutes,
four seconds."

He touched the index where the moth stood on the child's finger. The archive
had recorded the water darkening the cuff, one wing's torn edge, the tiny feet
testing skin. Nothing followed. No future leader, no discovery, no salvaged
species. The child shook the moth onto a sill and went to answer his father.

Meaningless, if meaning were permitted only to flow forward into famous
consequence.

The Recaller took the leaf from his mouth and crushed its coal against the
flywheel. A crescent of ash remained on the frost.

"Cancel Council selection. Index Nine."

"Authentication required."

He laid his palm on the spoke. A needle entered the old scar below his thumb.
The wheel tasted blood, heat, and salt.

"Authentication accepted," said the station. "Selection changed. This action
will be attributed."

"If attribution survives."

He pushed.

At tooth one hundred and eight his right knee buckled. At one hundred and
fifty, a bearing deep in the shaft began to scream. The sound did not resemble
machinery. It resembled an animal behind a closed door, and each turn made it
higher. Metal dust silvered his hands. The lightwell shutters crossed their
old tracks, reversing toward Vault Nine; through the window their black edges
entered the Jupiter glow like eclipsing moons.

At 04:26 the planet's limb ignited. Dawn came from behind Jupiter as a knife of
unfiltered sun, grazing the station nearly horizontal. It found every scratch
in the glass. It struck the Recaller at ankle height and climbed him as the
Archive rotated: shins, belt, ribs, mouth. His sweat became gold. His shadow
rose up the wall taller than the wheel.

"Forty teeth," said the station. "Particle arrival in two minutes."

The flywheel seized.

He pulled. He kicked the ratchet housing. He drove both hands between two
spokes and felt the tendons in his forearms start to tear. The wheel did not
move. Behind its hub a line of amber lubricant, thickened by cold, quivered
but would not fall.

Then the station said, very softly, "The council requests explanation."

The Recaller put his forehead against the iron. It smelled of old oil, ozone,
and his blood.

"Tell them the great events already know how to return," he said. "They have
statues, enemies, descendants, scars. Save what has no other witness."

"That is not a technical explanation."

"No. It is the mechanism."

At 04:28:11 he remembered the crescent of warm ash.

He scraped it from the spoke with one blackened thumbnail and packed it into
the frozen bearing race. Carbon, oil, water, salt: a filthy wedge where clean
lubricant had become glass. He rocked the wheel backward one fraction of a
tooth. The bearing gave a dull internal knock.

Then it turned.

He spent the last thirty-nine teeth without breath. The dawn line reached his
eyes. Their reflected light went white and almost cruel. The final tooth fell
at 04:28:57.

Around the lightwell, nine continental shutters met over Vault Nine. Their
seams closed from the outside inward until Jupiter became eight blades, four,
two, and none. Darkness struck. The bearing scream ceased. For one second the
only sound was the Recaller trying to breathe.

The radiation front arrived.

Blue fire ran over the outer hull. Dead systems spoke once. The wall filled
with error glyphs and went black. Through eleven kilometers of shutter metal
came a deep, soft concussion, too immense to be called a blow. Vaults One
through Eight received the white storm directly. Towers of war and theorem,
empire and engine, masterpiece and map, flashed into random heat.

Vault Nine remained.

In its shielded dark, an unbaked thumbprint endured. A snail crossed a mark. A
woman almost remembered a room. On a sill under equatorial rain, one damaged
moth opened its wings.

The Recaller sat beneath the stopped wheel. His hands would not close. Smoke
from the dead leaf still lingered in the shaft, thinner now, invisible except
where a single red lamp had survived.

At 04:31 station time, with the grand story burning around him, he began to
count what the universe had nearly failed to notice.
