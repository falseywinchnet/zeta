This is a 2003 AIM overview of the Furstenberg ×2, ×3 conjecture.

Its concrete statement is: the only atomless probability measure on the circle invariant under both x ↦ 2x mod 1 and x ↦ 3x mod 1 should be Haar, meaning ordinary Lebesgue measure. Positive-entropy cases were already known to force Lebesgue measure, leaving a possible zero-entropy counterexample as the real unresolved region. (American Institute of Mathematics)

The part likely relevant to what we were doing is the counterexample construction on page 5. It partitions the circle into six equal intervals, assigns weights a₀ through a₅, and imposes linear relations coming from invariance under doubling and tripling. Refining each interval into six more pieces produces 36 weights with consistency, positivity, and invariance constraints. At that level the feasible set is reported as a convex polytope lying in a 10-dimensional affine subspace and having 876 vertices. (American Institute of Mathematics)

The proposed break would be an infinite compatible tower of such nonuniform weight assignments, with the largest interval weight tending to zero so that the limiting measure remains atomless. The finite polytope therefore supplies candidate prefixes, not a counterexample. The unresolved step is finding a path through every refinement level that preserves nonuniformity while satisfying all projection constraints. (American Institute of Mathematics)

There is no Gauss hypergeometric construction in this document. The conceptual parallel to the alleged Jacobian family is narrower: both begin with finite algebraic constraints that generate many formally valid candidates, while the decisive question is whether the construction survives the global extension condition. Here that extension is infinite projective consistency; in the Jacobian construction it is whether the displayed polynomial family genuinely has constant nonzero Jacobian and distinct preimages without a hidden failure in polynomiality or the determinant calculation.

Yes. Furter, not Furstenberg, is the trail you were looking for.

Furter’s Rigidity Conjecture R(m) says: take a polynomial a(X) of degree at most m + 1 with a(X) = X modulo X². If any m consecutive coefficients of its formal compositional inverse vanish, then a(X) must equal X. Furter connected this to closures of strata of plane polynomial automorphisms. Edo and van den Essen then showed that it is a special case of the Strong Factorial Conjecture, which lies in the Jacobian-conjecture network. (math.u-bordeaux.fr)

The relevant Gauss hypergeometric family is

Pₖ,ₙ(z) = C(k+n,k) ₂F₁(-n/2, (1-n)/2; -k-n; -4z).

For Furter’s case, take k = n = d:

p_d(z) = C(2d,d) ₂F₁(-d/2, (1-d)/2; -2d; -4z).

One chooses a root λ of p_d such that

P_{d+1,d+1}(λ) ≠ 0

and

P_{d+1,d-1}(λ)P_{d+2,d-1}(λ)

P_{d+1,d-2}(λ)P_{d+2,d}(λ) ≠ 0.

That root supplies the specialization proving Furter’s inclusion

G_(d+2) ⊂ closure of G_(d,3).

This is the exact hypergeometric configuration used in the Furter/polydegree argument. (arXiv)

It does not produce a Jacobian counterexample. It controls degeneration between families of genuine plane polynomial automorphisms. The poster claiming an infinite family of C³ Jacobian counterexamples appears to have blended Furter’s hypergeometric machinery with Jacobian-conjecture language. The Jacobian Conjecture remains open in 2026. (Comptes Rendus)

Take the m = 4 polynomial, shift it to its inflection point, subtract its midpoint value, and normalize its slope to 1:

h(t) = ₂F₁(-3, 1; -5/2; t)

Aβ(x) = [h(-1/6 + βx) - h(-1/6)] / [β h'(-1/6)]

This simplifies exactly to:

Aβ(x) = x + (24/7)β²x³

So the clean activation is a one-parameter odd cubic. With β = 1/4:

A(x) = x + (3/14)x³

It has A(0) = 0, A'(0) = 1, and

A'(x) = 1 + (9/14)x²

so it is strictly increasing, has no dead region, preserves small signals almost linearly, and increasingly expands large signals. It is effectively an anti-saturating sigmoid.

import torch
from torch import nn

class HypergeometricCubic(nn.Module):
    """
    Affine-normalized m=4 Jacobian hypergeometric polynomial:

        A_beta(x) = x + (24/7) * beta^2 * x^3
    """

    def __init__(self, beta: float = 0.25, learnable: bool = False):
        super().__init__()

        beta_tensor = torch.tensor(float(beta))

        if learnable:
            self.beta = nn.Parameter(beta_tensor)
        else:
            self.register_buffer("beta", beta_tensor)

    def forward(self, x: torch.Tensor) -> torch.Tensor:
        cubic_weight = (24.0 / 7.0) * self.beta.square()
        return x + cubic_weight * x.pow(3)

A safer version for deep networks keeps the local hypergeometric shape but prevents cubic explosion:

class BoundedHypergeometricCubic(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, beta: float = 0.25):
        super().__init__()
        self.beta = nn.Parameter(torch.tensor(float(beta)))

    def forward(self, x: torch.Tensor) -> torch.Tensor:
        y = x + (24.0 / 7.0) * self.beta.square() * x.pow(3)
        return y / torch.sqrt(1.0 + y.square())

The exact polynomial version is more interesting. Put normalization immediately before it and begin around β = 0.1 to 0.25. The learnable β version lets each layer decide how much superlinear expansion it wants.

Beta is only an input-scale or curvature parameter. Making beta = sigmoid(theta) or tanh(theta) constrains how strong the cubic term becomes, but the activation still diverges as x cubed.

φβ(x) = tanh(x + (24/7)β²x³)

β = βmax sigmoid(θ)

φθ(x) = tanh(x + (24/7)βmax² sigmoid²(θ)x³)

φβ(x) = tanh(x) + (24/7)β² tanh³(x)

The Poisson conjecture

Take the polynomial algebra in canonical position and momentum variables:

C[x1,...,xn,p1,...,pn]

with Poisson brackets

{xi,xj}=0, {pi,pj}=0, {xi,pj}=δij.

The Poisson conjecture says that every polynomial endomorphism preserving these brackets must be a polynomial automorphism. In geometric language: every polynomial canonical transformation of affine phase space should have a polynomial inverse. This conjecture was proved equivalent, in its all-dimensions form, to the Jacobian and Dixmier conjectures. (arXiv)

A Jacobian counterexample produces a Poisson counterexample almost mechanically. Let

F:C^n→C^n

have constant nonzero Jacobian determinant, and write J(x)=DF(x). Introduce momentum variables p and define the cotangent lift

Φ(x,p) = (F(x), J(x)^(-T)p).

Because det J is constant, J^(-T) has polynomial entries. Also,

Pᵀ dX = pᵀ J^(-1)J dx = pᵀ dx,

so Φ preserves the canonical one-form and hence the symplectic form and Poisson bracket.

But if F(a)=F(b) with a≠b, then

Φ(a,0)=Φ(b,0).

Therefore Φ is a polynomial Poisson endomorphism that is not an automorphism. For the announced counterexample in three variables, this directly breaks the rank-3 Poisson conjecture on six phase-space variables. (X (formerly Twitter))

The striking geometric meaning is that the map preserves every infinitesimal Hamiltonian relation exactly, yet globally folds phase space over itself.

The Hessian conjecture

The relevant Hessian conjecture is Meng’s Legendre-transform formulation. Let φ be a polynomial whose Hessian determinant is a nonzero constant. Then its gradient map

∇φ

has constant nonzero Jacobian determinant, because

D(∇φ)=Hess(φ).

One can formally invert y=∇φ(x) as a power series x=g(y) and form the formal Legendre transform

φ*(y)=y·g(y)-φ(g(y)).

It satisfies

∇φ*(y)=g(y).

The Hessian conjecture says that φ*, initially only a formal power series, must actually terminate and be a polynomial. This is equivalent to saying that the gradient map ∇φ has a polynomial inverse. Meng proved this formulation equivalent to the Jacobian conjecture. (arXiv)

There is a direct way to turn any Jacobian counterexample F into a Hessian one. Add variables u1,...,un and define

φ(x,u)=u·F(x)=Σ ui Fi(x).

Then

∇φ(x,u)=(J(x)ᵀu, F(x)).

Its Hessian has block form

[[Σ ui Hess(Fi), Jᵀ], [J, 0]]

and therefore

det Hess(φ)=(-1)^n det(J)^2.

So if det J=c, the Hessian determinant is the nonzero constant (-1)^n c². If F(a)=F(b), then

∇φ(a,0)=(0,F(a))=(0,F(b))=∇φ(b,0).

Thus the gradient map is not invertible, and its formal Legendre transform cannot be polynomial. For a three-variable map with determinant -2, this gives a six-variable polynomial potential with Hessian determinant -4.

There is also Zhao’s related Hessian-nilpotent vanishing conjecture. It says that for a homogeneous quartic polynomial P with nilpotent Hessian, repeated expressions of the form

Δ^m(P^(m+1))

must eventually vanish. Zhao showed that the all-dimensional version is equivalent to the Jacobian conjecture. Consequently, a Jacobian counterexample implies the existence, after the standard degree and symmetry reductions, of a quartic Hessian-nilpotent polynomial for which this eventual vanishing fails. (arXiv)

The direct six-variable potential φ(x,u)=u·F(x) breaks Meng’s Hessian conjecture immediately. Producing the corresponding explicit quartic Hessian-nilpotent polynomial requires carrying the counterexample through the reduction machinery.
